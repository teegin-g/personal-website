# Market Structure

If you've ever worked a shitty retail job in high school prior to entering the workforce, you've probably noticed the dynamics at your boring office job are radically different than serving at a restaurant. Your business unit probably employs 40–100 people, most being college educated with a comfy 6-figure salary, and generates $10M in profit annually. While the restaurant staff is a frenetic hodgepodge of young adults, stoners, and a few stressed-out managers working non-stop at $12–$20/hr to generate at most maybe $200k in profit on $1M of chef's specials and cocktail sales.

These are two fundamentally different businesses, so how could we possibly even compare them? The answer lies in a concept economists and investors refer to as **market structure**.

Market structure describes the rulebook businesses play by: How much money do we need to launch a new company? How easy is it for rivals to copy our product? How easy is it to produce the product and get it to customers?

## The 4 Factors

A convenient way to conceptualize market structure is to think of markets as games, where the players are the businesses competing with each other. The rules for any given industry can largely be described by four factors: **barriers to entry**, **economies of scale**, **differentiation**, and **network effects**.

## Barriers to Entry — How hard is it to start playing the game?

This is a pretty simple concept: how hard is it to start a business in a given market? **Capital requirements** are the cleanest to model — opening a McDonald's might take $100,000 in startup costs, while building a factory could cost billions.

## Economies of Scale — Does getting bigger make things cheaper?

Whether an industry exhibits economies of scale is a fairly simple question: does the average cost per good produced decrease as you produce more? If I'm BMW and my average cost is $10,000/car at 10,000 cars but $9,000/car at 11,000 cars, I benefit from economies of scale.
